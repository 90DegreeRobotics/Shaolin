# Pinyin and the Sound System
## The Complete Foundation for Pronunciation and Tones

Pinyin is the romanization system for Mandarin. It is not "Chinese in English
letters" — several letters are false friends. Learn the sound system once,
correctly, and every word you ever learn afterward is built on rock. Learn it
sloppily and every card in your deck is quietly corrupted.

Every Mandarin syllable has three parts:

```text
syllable = initial (optional) + final + tone
mā = m + a + tone 1
```

There are only about 400 base syllables. With tones, about 1,300. That is the
entire sound inventory of the language. It is finite and it can be drilled.

## The Four Tones (Plus Neutral)

Tones are not decoration. `mā má mǎ mà` are four different words
(mother / hemp / horse / scold). A wrong tone is a wrong word.

| Tone | Mark | Contour | Feel | Example |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1st | ā | high, flat, held (55) | singing a steady note | 妈 mā (mother) |
| 2nd | á | rising (35) | asking "what?" in English | 麻 má (hemp) |
| 3rd | ǎ | low, dipping (21(4)) | a low croak; drops and stays low | 马 mǎ (horse) |
| 4th | à | sharp fall (51) | a firm command: "No." | 骂 mà (scold) |
| neutral | a | short, light, unstressed | a throwaway syllable | 吗 ma (question particle) |

Physical drill: trace the contour with your hand in the air as you say the
syllable. High flat sweep for 1st, rise for 2nd, dip for 3rd, chop for 4th.
The body teaches the ear.

**The truth about 3rd tone:** in real speech it is usually just a *low* tone.
The full dip-and-rise only appears when the syllable stands alone or at the end
of a phrase. Practice it as "low and held."

### Tone Change Rules (Tone Sandhi)

These are mandatory, not stylistic:

1. **3rd + 3rd → 2nd + 3rd.** Two 3rd tones in a row: the first becomes 2nd.
   你好 nǐ hǎo is pronounced *ní hǎo*.
2. **不 bù becomes bú before a 4th tone.** 不是 bú shì, 不去 bú qù.
   Otherwise it stays bù: 不好 bù hǎo.
3. **一 yī changes by context.** Alone or counting: yī. Before a 4th tone: yí
   (一次 yí cì). Before 1st/2nd/3rd tones: yì (一天 yì tiān, 一年 yì nián).

### Tone Mark Placement

The mark goes on the main vowel: **a** first; if no a, then **o** or **e**;
in `iu` and `ui`, the mark goes on the *second* letter (liù, huì).

## The Initials (Syllable Beginnings)

Grouped by mouth position. Approximations are training wheels — replace them
with your ear as soon as audio enters the routine.

| Group | Letters | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Lips | b, p, m, f | as in English; b is unaspirated (no puff), p has a strong puff |
| Tongue tip | d, t, n, l | as in English; d unaspirated, t puffed |
| Throat | g, k, h | g unaspirated; h is rougher than English h, slightly raspy |
| **Palatals** | j, q, x | tongue tip DOWN behind lower teeth, lips spread wide |
| **Retroflex** | zh, ch, sh, r | tongue tip curled UP toward the roof of the mouth |
| Dentals | z, c, s | z = "ds" in *kids*; c = "ts" in *cats* — never a k sound |
| Glides | y, w | spelling devices for i- and u- finals at syllable start |

### The Two Deadly Pairs

Almost every English speaker collapses these. Do not.

**j / q / x versus zh / ch / sh.** Same-ish consonant idea, opposite tongue.
- j q x: tongue tip anchored *down* behind the lower teeth, smile position.
  They can only be followed by i or ü sounds. 京 jīng, 气 qì, 心 xīn.
- zh ch sh: tongue tip curled *up* and back. 中 zhōng, 茶 chá, 是 shì.
- Drill pair: 下 xià (down) vs 夏 shà — no: use 想 xiǎng vs 上 shàng.

**c versus ch.** c = flat "ts" (菜 cài); ch = curled tongue (吃 chī).

### r

Mandarin r (日 rì, 人 rén) is not English r. Tongue curled like sh, but voiced —
somewhere between English "r" and the "s" in *measure*. Do not round your lips.

## The Finals (Syllable Endings)

### Simple finals

| Final | Sound | Example |
| --- | --- | --- |
| a | "ah" | 妈 mā |
| o | "aw" with rounded lips | 我 wǒ |
| e | unrounded "uh" (throat sound, NOT "eh") | 喝 hē |
| i | "ee" — BUT see the empty rime below | 你 nǐ |
| u | "oo" | 路 lù |
| ü | "ee" with tightly rounded lips (German ü, French u) | 绿 lǜ |
| er | "ar" with curled tongue | 二 èr |

**The empty rime:** after zh, ch, sh, r, z, c, s, the letter *i* is NOT "ee."
It is a buzzed continuation of the consonant: 是 shì ≈ "shrr," 字 zì ≈ "dzz,"
四 sì ≈ "szz." This is one of the most common beginner corruption points.

**ü in disguise:** after j, q, x, y the two dots are dropped but the sound
remains ü: 去 qù, 句 jù, 需 xū, 雨 yǔ are ALL the ü sound. Only after n and l
are the dots written (女 nǚ, 绿 lǜ), because nu/lu also exist.

### Compound finals

| Family | Finals | Trap notes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| a-family | ai, ao, an, ang | an = "ahn," ang = "ahng" with throat open |
| e-family | ei, en, eng | ei = "ay"; en = "un" in *fun*; eng = "ung" |
| o-family | ou, ong | ou = "oh"; ong = "oong" |
| i-family | ia, ie, iao, iu, ian, in, iang, ing, iong | ie = "yeh"; **ian = "yen," not "yan"**; iu = abbreviated iou ("yo") |
| u-family | ua, uo, uai, ui, uan, un, uang | ui = abbreviated uei ("way"); un = abbreviated uen ("wun") |
| ü-family | üe, üan, ün | üan = "ü-en"; after j/q/x written without dots: 元 yuán |

### -n versus -ng

The second great corruption point. -n: tongue tip touches behind the teeth
(as in *sin*). -ng: tongue stays back, throat open (as in *sing*).
Minimal drill pairs: 分 fēn / 风 fēng · 心 xīn / 星 xīng · 判 pàn / 胖 pàng.

### Spelling conventions (why y and w appear)

When an i-, u-, or ü-final has no initial, the spelling changes but not the
sound: i → yi, in → yin, u → wu, uo → wo, ü → yu, üan → yuan. These are
spelling rules, not new sounds.

## The Tone-Pair Grid

Words are mostly two syllables, so tones are drilled in *pairs* — all 20
combinations. This grid is your speaking gym for the first months. Say each
aloud daily; the goal is that any pair feels as automatic as a stance.

| Pair | Example | Pinyin | Meaning |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1+1 | 今天 | jīntiān | today |
| 1+2 | 中国 | Zhōngguó | China |
| 1+3 | 铅笔 | qiānbǐ | pencil |
| 1+4 | 工夫 | gōngfu* | effort/time (*often neutral) |
| 1+0 | 妈妈 | māma | mom |
| 2+1 | 明天 | míngtiān | tomorrow |
| 2+2 | 学习 | xuéxí | to study |
| 2+3 | 牛奶 | niúnǎi | milk |
| 2+4 | 白菜 | báicài | bok choy |
| 2+0 | 学生 | xuésheng | student |
| 3+1 | 老师 | lǎoshī | teacher |
| 3+2 | 起床 | qǐchuáng | get out of bed |
| 3+3 | 你好 | nǐhǎo | hello (says ní hǎo) |
| 3+4 | 马步 | mǎbù | horse stance |
| 3+0 | 早上 | zǎoshang | morning |
| 4+1 | 气功 | qìgōng | qi gong |
| 4+2 | 练拳 | liànquán | practice boxing |
| 4+3 | 汉语 | Hànyǔ | Chinese language |
| 4+4 | 静坐 | jìngzuò | sit in stillness |
| 4+0 | 谢谢 | xièxie | thanks |

Note the training words hiding in the grid: 马步, 气功, 练拳, 静坐. Your
tone drill is already Kung Fu vocabulary.

## The 10-Day Pinyin Bootcamp

Run this once at the start (or as remediation any time the foundation feels
soft). 10-15 minutes per day, always ALOUD, ideally with any slow audio source
from [RESOURCES.md](RESOURCES.md) for ear-checking.

```text
Day 1:  Four tones on single syllables: ma, ba, da. Hand gestures.
Day 2:  Tones continued + neutral tone. Record yourself once.
Day 3:  Simple finals: a o e i u ü. The e and ü sounds.
Day 4:  b p m f / d t n l + finals. Aspiration (puff/no puff).
Day 5:  g k h / z c s. The "ts" of c. Empty rime: zi ci si.
Day 6:  j q x. Tongue DOWN. ji qi xi, jia qia xia, ju qu xu (= ü!).
Day 7:  zh ch sh r. Tongue UP. zhi chi shi ri. Contrast day 6 vs day 7.
Day 8:  Compound finals: ai ei ao ou an en ang eng ong. -n vs -ng pairs.
Day 9:  i/u/ü families: ian, iu, ui, un, üe, üan. Spelling rules.
Day 10: Tone pairs. Read the 20-pair grid aloud twice. Record and compare
        to day 2. Keep both recordings.
```

## The Daily 5-Minute Drill (Permanent)

After the bootcamp, this becomes the "Morning Mouth" block of the
[Temple Day](../TEMPLE_DAY.md):

```text
1. One tone-pair row of the grid, 3 times aloud. (1 min)
2. One trap contrast: j/q/x vs zh/ch/sh, or -n vs -ng, or c vs ch. (1 min)
3. Shadow one slow sentence from your audio source: listen, repeat,
   listen, repeat. (2 min)
4. Say this week's journal character aloud with its tone, 5 times. (1 min)
```

## Corruption Checks

Monthly, test yourself honestly:

- Can I hear the difference between 想 xiǎng and 上 shàng blind?
- Do I say 是 shì with a buzz, or an English "ee"?
- Is my 去 qù an ü, or an "oo"?
- Does my 2nd tone actually rise, or does it flatten when I speak fast?
- Do I still convert 你好 to ní hǎo automatically?

A no on any of these means one remediation day from the bootcamp. Foundation
first. Speed never outranks accuracy — a fast wrong tone is a wrong word said
confidently.
