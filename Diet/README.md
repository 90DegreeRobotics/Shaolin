# The Diet Lane — Food as Training

This is the food discipline for the one-year path. It is a lane, like the
[Mandarin Study Hall](../Mandarin/README.md): a governed practice, not a purity
contest. It is the corpus the Master (Chirox) grounds in when it speaks about
what to eat. The Master cites these sections; it does not invent nutrition.

## Standing

Food is fuel, recovery, and mood regulation — not a moral scoreboard (manual
§"Food, Hydration, and Stimulants: Yi as Medicine"). This lane is **plant-forward
and pragmatic**: the plate is built on plants, and conscious animal protein is
allowed and used deliberately to fuel hard training. The aim is a calm,
predictable pattern that does not agitate the mind or overload digestion, so the
body is free for practice.

This project is not affiliated with any Shaolin temple, lineage, or teacher, and
this lane is not medical or dietary prescription. It is a disciplined default a
practitioner can adopt, scale, and — where a real medical condition or very high
training load is involved — adapt with qualified help. Discipline, not dogma.

## Guiding Principles

- **Simple and predictable.** Few ingredients, clean preparation, little strong
  seasoning. The taste comes from freshness and variety, not from heat and sauce.
- **Heavy at midday, light at night.** The largest meal near the main training
  block; a light dinner that does not sit on top of sleep.
- **Plant-forward base, conscious protein.** Grains, beans, tofu/tempeh,
  vegetables, fruit, nuts, and seeds are the floor. Eggs, fish, or lean meat are
  used deliberately when the training load asks for it — chosen, not drifted into.
- **Minimal processing.** No ultraprocessed snacks, no sugary drinks, no alcohol
  as fuel. Water and plain tea between meals rather than during, to keep digestion
  smooth.
- **The meal is a practice.** How you eat trains attention as much as what you eat.

## The Base Pantry

Keep a fixed, boring, reliable pantry so choice fatigue never becomes an excuse.

The full concrete catalog — every food by tier, meal templates, the congee
formula, training-day snacks, the protein cheat sheet, and the weekly shopping
skeleton — lives in [FOODS.md](FOODS.md). This section is the summary; that
file is the list you shop and cook from.

- **Grains:** rice, oats, barley, millet, whole-wheat noodles, simple bread.
- **Legumes / protein:** lentils, chickpeas, black/adzuki beans, tofu, tempeh,
  edamame; eggs, fish, or lean meat as your conscious protein when needed.
- **Vegetables:** five or six kinds across the day; seasonal and plain-cooked.
- **Fruit:** whole fruit, especially around training.
- **Fats:** nuts, seeds, a little olive oil; whole sources over heavy oils.
- **Excluded by default:** ultraprocessed snacks, sugary drinks, alcohol, heavy
  sauces, deep-fried food. Not as sins — as friction the practice does not need.

## The Three Meals

- **Breakfast (early, before or after first practice):** grain- and bean-based.
  An "eight-treasures" congee — multiple grains, a bean, nuts, seeds, a little
  dried fruit — gives slow-release energy for morning work.
- **Lunch (the main meal, near midday):** rice or grain, a full protein (tofu,
  beans, eggs, or fish), and five or six vegetables. This is where most of the
  day's calories and protein land.
- **Dinner (light):** noodles or bread with simple vegetables and a little
  protein — enough to replenish, not enough to burden sleep.

## Portioning and Hunger

- **The 80% rule.** Stop before dullness, at roughly eight-tenths full (manual:
  "Stop before dullness. Overeating steals clarity."). Leave a little room.
- **Recalibrate slowly.** If you tend to overeat, start a meal with half to
  two-thirds of your usual amount, finish it, and wait before deciding on more.
- **Eat slowly.** Chew thoroughly, set the utensils down between bites, give the
  meal real minutes. Speed is where overeating and distraction live.
- **Name the hunger.** Learn to tell true hunger from habit and emotional craving.
  This is the same attention you train in meditation, applied to the plate.

## Timing and Daily Rhythm

- Breakfast early; the main intake at midday near the hard training block; a light
  dinner that stops a few hours before sleep.
- Water and tea **between** meals, not constantly during them.
- Hold a steady sleep window (the manual's 7.5–8 hours). Food timing serves the
  sleep, not the reverse.

## Fueling the Forge

This is the honest part, and it is not softness — the Master does not lie about
the body. A clean, restrained diet stacked on **hard, progressive training must be
actively fueled**, or recovery collapses and the year stalls. Under-fueling a
forge is the fire that destroys, not the one that forges.

- **Protein every meal.** Combine legumes, soy, and grains; add eggs or fish when
  the load rises. Aim to hit real daily protein, not a token amount.
- **Carbohydrate for the work.** Whole grains and fruit around training fuel
  high-intensity effort and refill glycogen. Restriction and hard training do not
  belong on the same day without a reason.
- **Watch the plant-forward gaps.** If you lean heavily off animal foods, be
  deliberate about **B12, iron, omega-3, and total energy** — these are the ones
  that quietly fail a hard-training vegetarian.
- **Snack with intent when volume climbs.** Fruit and nuts before or after a big
  session beats enlarging the main meals.

The 80% rule governs *dullness and greed*, not *fueling the work*. Discipline is
eating clean and slow; stupidity is training hard on empty. Keep them separate.

## Starting Overweight or Deconditioned

If you begin this path carrying excess weight, weak, and out of condition, the
fueling advice above bends — but does not break. You are not fueling high-volume
temple training yet; you are rebuilding a neglected body while shedding what it
does not need. Both can happen at once, and for an untrained beginner they often
do.

- **A moderate deficit is correct here, not a betrayal of the path.** Eat somewhat
  less than you burn so fat comes off — but *moderate*. Slow and steady beats
  heroic starvation, which strips muscle, wrecks recovery, and rebounds. A crash
  diet is the fire that destroys.
- **Hold protein high.** This is the one non-negotiable. Adequate protein while in
  a deficit lets you lose fat and still build the strength a beginner's body gains
  quickly. Skimp on protein and you lose muscle you cannot spare.
- **Fat loss and strength gain coexist early.** A deconditioned starter can do both
  for months before the body demands you pick one. Use that window; do not waste it
  by eating like a monk who trains six hours a day when you do not yet.
- **The scale is a slow signal, not a daily verdict.** Weight lies day to day
  (water, food, sleep). Read the trend over weeks in the ledger, the way you read
  stance duration — a line, not a point. Do not negotiate with a single morning.
- **The Beginner Rule governs the body, and it governs the plate too.** Begin lower
  than pride wants. The first victory is showing up tomorrow, having eaten enough
  protein and not enough junk to hate yourself. Consistency now outweighs perfection.

Over the four quarters this eases: as conditioning rises and the training earns
more food, the deficit narrows toward maintenance and the plate grows to match the
work. You are not on a diet. You are learning to feed a body that trains.

## The Four-Quarter Arc

The lane ramps with the training phases (progressive load). Each quarter refines
the last; you earn the tightening rather than forcing it on Day 1.

### Quarter 1: Stabilize

Fix the container. Set three meal times and hold them. Remove the obvious junk —
sugary drinks, ultraprocessed snacks, alcohol as fuel. Move the plate plant-forward
(roughly vegetables : protein : grains as 1:1:2). Goal: predictability, not
restriction. Training is still moderate; eat to build the habit.

### Quarter 2: Cleanse

Simplify. Cut the heavy and overstimulating — deep-fried food, sugary snacks,
strong sauces — and notice how simpler food changes the mind and the training.
Reduce, don't crash. As training volume rises into the deepen phase, hold protein
and carbohydrate up even as you simplify.

### Quarter 3: Sharpen

Tighten discipline. Bring eating into set daytime windows aligned to training,
hold portion sizes consistent, and demand full attention at every meal. This is
the willpower quarter — but the hardest training is here too, so **sharpen the
rules, not the calories**: fuel the work, restrain the drift.

### Quarter 4: Sustain

Prove independence. Hold the pattern without a scaffold. Introduce small,
deliberate deviations only when you can observe them fully and return without
sliding. This is balance, not asceticism — the mark that food has become quiet
and lived rather than a daily fight.

## The Eating Meditation

Once a week, eat one meal in full silence, no screen, no book: only the food, the
breath, and attention. Know exactly what is in it. Chew, set the utensils down,
receive it. This ties the plate directly to the contemplative practice — a mindful
meal is a training session for presence and restraint.

## On Tradition

Strict monastic Shaolin/Buddhist eating goes further than this lane: fully
vegetarian, and avoiding the "five pungents" (garlic, onions, chives, leeks,
scallions) because tradition holds they excite the emotions and disturb
meditation. **That is a spiritual and traditional practice, not a nutritional
fact**, and this pragmatic lane does not require it. If you choose to adopt it as
a discipline, adopt it knowingly, as tradition — not because a machine told you an
onion is unhealthy. It isn't.

## Sourcing and Honesty

The disciplinary structure here — three meals, midday-heavy rhythm, the 80% rule,
mindful eating, a clean base pantry, and a quarterly progression — is sound and
consistent with this project's manual. Specific "monks eat exactly X" claims that
circulate online vary in reliability; where this lane states a tradition, it labels
it a tradition. Where it states a fueling point (protein, B12, energy under load),
that is basic sports nutrition, not lineage secret. Verify anything you intend to
lean your health on, and adapt with qualified help if you carry a medical
condition or a very high training load.
