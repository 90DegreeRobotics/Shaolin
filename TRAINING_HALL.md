# The Training Hall — 练功房 liàngōngfáng
## Every Exercise, Every Stretch, Every Tool — Traditional, Improvised, and Purchased

This document is the complete physical inventory of the path: everything we
train now, the traditional Shaolin training implements and when a practitioner
earns them, how to build honest substitutes from what a household already
holds, and exactly what to buy when buying becomes justified.

Three standing rules govern all of it:

1. **The Pain Rule and the Beginner Rule** (manual, "Entering the Path")
   outrank every tool and every tradition on this page.
2. **功 gōng is earned, not purchased.** Kung Fu (功夫 gōngfu) means skill
   through time and work. A garage full of equipment trains nothing. Every
   tool below exists to add *resistance to a skill you already practice
   empty-handed* — never to replace the practice.
3. **Tradition is labeled as tradition.** Where a claim is folk history or
   legend, it is named as such. Honoring a culture means not laundering its
   legends into facts — and not stripping its language off its methods.
   The Chinese names are kept because they are the real names.

This project is not affiliated with the Shaolin Temple or any lineage.
This is a practitioner's reconstruction from openly documented practice.

# Part I — The Exercise Catalog (What We Train)

Everything currently in the system: the manual's curriculum, the Temple Day
blocks, and every drill Chirox's Mirror can measure. Phase numbers refer to
the manual's 12-month blueprint.

## 1. Sitting and Breath — 禅 chán

| Practice | Chinese | Method | When |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Breath meditation | 打坐 dǎzuò | Sit upright, feel the breath, return without commentary. 3–25 min. | Phase 1, daily, forever |
| Seated posture (measured) | 坐禅 zuòchán | The Mirror checks: spine tall, shoulders level, settled on the seat. | any sit |
| The pause | — | Feel the first spark of reaction before speech or action. | Phase 2 onward, all day |

## 2. Qi Gong — 气功 qìgōng

**Standing foundation:**

| Practice | Chinese | Method | When |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Wuji standing (measured) | 无极桩 wújí zhuāng | Stand empty and tall, arms hanging, suspended from the crown. Start 2–3 min. | Phase 1 |
| Holding Up the Heavens (measured) | 双手托天 | Arms press the sky, legs rooted; the Mirror checks arm extension and root. 10–15 slow reps. | Phase 1 |

**The Eight Brocades — 八段锦 bā duàn jǐn** (full set from Phase 2; movement
one alone in Phase 1). Each movement pairs breath with a stretch-and-press
pattern; the traditional names carry the intent:

| # | Movement | Chinese | What it works |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | Two Hands Hold Up the Heavens | 双手托天理三焦 | full-body lengthening, breath expansion |
| 2 | Drawing the Bow to Shoot the Hawk | 左右开弓似射雕 | horse stance endurance, chest and upper back |
| 3 | Separate Heaven and Earth | 调理脾胃须单举 | opposing single-arm press, trunk lines |
| 4 | The Wise Owl Gazes Backward | 五劳七伤往后瞧 | neck and thoracic rotation, posture reset |
| 5 | Sway the Head, Swing the Tail | 摇头摆尾去心火 | deep horse stance, hip and spine circling |
| 6 | Two Hands Hold the Feet | 两手攀足固肾腰 | posterior chain fold, lower back care |
| 7 | Clench the Fists, Glare with Eyes | 攒拳怒目增气力 | horse stance punching, grip, focused intent |
| 8 | Seven Bounces Shake Off Illness | 背后七颠百病消 | heel raises and settling vibration, closing |

The organ-healing claims in the names (三焦 the Triple Burner, heart-fire,
kidney strength) are traditional Chinese medicine framing — kept because they
are the names, practiced because the movements are excellent regardless.

## 3. The Stance Curriculum — 桩功 zhuānggōng

The stances are the school. Every one below is measured live by the Mirror
(front or side camera), which is why they are listed with their truth-checks.

| Stance | Chinese | The Mirror checks | When |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Horse Stance | 马步 mǎbù | knees toward 90°, trunk stacked, no lean | Phase 1, day 1 |
| Horse Stance, guard up | 马步抱拳 | the horse plus fists at chest height | Phase 1–2 |
| Bow Stance | 弓步 gōngbù | front knee bent over foot, rear leg long | Phase 2 |
| Drop Stance (shallow) | 仆步 púbù | one leg sinks, one extends — split levels, trunk tall | Phase 2–3, scaled |
| Empty / Cat Stance | 虚步 xūbù | rear leg carries all weight, front foot empty | after balance improves |
| Crane / One-Leg Stand | 独立步 dúlìbù | standing knee tall, raised knee above hip line | Phase 2 |
| Parallel Ready Stance | 预备式 yùbèishì | knees soft — ready, not locked, not squatting | Phase 1 |
| Narrow Meditation Stance | — | quiet tall standing, spine centered | Phase 1 |
| T-Stance, arms level | — | legs together and straight, arms a level crossbeam | Phase 2 |

Stance work is accumulated, not endured in one heroic hold: 5 rounds of 20
seconds honest beats 100 seconds collapsing. Depth is earned by alignment.

## 4. Fundamental Leg Work — 基本功 jīběngōng

The traditional daily stretching-and-kicking set, done after warming up,
before strength. Order: presses first, then kicks, splits progression last.

**Presses and openers (stretches):**

| Stretch | Chinese | Method |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Front leg press | 正压腿 zhèng yātuǐ | heel on support at hip height (chair back, rail); hips square; fold the chest toward the shin, spine long. 8–10 slow presses per leg |
| Side leg press | 侧压腿 cè yātuǐ | same support, body turned 90°; ear reaches toward the raised leg |
| Back leg press | 后压腿 hòu yātuǐ | instep on support behind you; stretch the front of the hip. Gentle — this one is earned |
| Drop-stance press | 仆步压腿 púbù yātuǐ | in shallow Pu Bu, shift weight over the bent leg, press the long leg's line |
| Shoulder press | 压肩 yājiān | hands on a support at hip height, step back, press the chest down through straight arms — the classic rail-press the yard does before staff work |
| Waist circling | 涮腰 shuànyāo | feet wide, circle the trunk fully around the hips, both directions |
| Hip opening | 开胯 kāikuà | seated butterfly, soles together, knees pressed gently toward the floor |
| Split progressions | 竖叉 shùchā (front) / 横叉 héngchā (side) | patient daily inches; the year's goal is progress, not the floor |

**Kicks (dynamic stretches — leg swings with a straight trunk):**

| Kick | Chinese | Method |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Front stretch kick | 正踢腿 zhèng tītuǐ | straight leg swings to the forehead line; trunk does not fold |
| Side kick swing | 侧踢腿 cè tītuǐ | straight leg swings up beside the ear line |
| Inside crescent | 里合腿 lǐhétuǐ | leg circles from outside to inside across the body |
| Outside crescent | 外摆腿 wàibǎituǐ | leg circles from inside to outside |
| Snap kick | 弹腿 tántuǐ | knee lifts, lower leg snaps out relaxed — speed without force |
| Heel kick | 蹬腿 dēngtuǐ | slow pressing kick with the heel, hips square |

Rule for all of it: 8–10 per side, height earned not forced, counted in
Mandarin (一, 二, 三…).

## 5. Conditioning — 排打之前先有力

Strength before hardening. Every drill below is measured or counted by the
Mirror.

**Measured holds:** plank / pushup top (straight line, no sag), wall sit
(thighs toward parallel), squat hold, hollow body hold, glute bridge hold,
leg raise hold.

**Counted reps:** slow squats, pushups (scaled — wall, incline, knees,
full), situps, knee raises, low-impact jumping jacks.

**From the manual's Phase 2 body work:** rows (when equipment exists),
loaded carries, core bracing, basic stepping between stances.

**Locomotion:** the Daoist observation walk (20 min, no devices), stair
work, hiking (Phase 3 Saturday sessions), and — as conditioning rises —
the morning run in the temple pattern (跑山 pǎoshān, "running the mountain,"
scaled to a deconditioned start: walk hills first).

## 6. Recovery Work

Walking, mobility for ankles/hips/thoracic spine/shoulders, gentle Qi Gong,
breath work, and the deload week every 6–8 weeks. Recovery is training
(manual, "Recovery Discipline").

# Part II — The Traditional Armory — 器械 qìxiè

What the training yard actually uses, what each tool is *for*, and when a
practitioner traditionally earns it. The old sequence is unambiguous:
**years of stances, flexibility, and basics come before any implement.**
A child at a temple school touches nothing but the floor and their own legs
for a very long time. The tools below enter in roughly the order listed.

The context for many of them is the tradition of the **72 Arts of Shaolin
(少林七十二艺 qīshí'èr yì)** — a catalog of specialized hard and soft
conditioning methods (iron palm, eagle claw, iron shirt, and the rest).
Treat the 72 Arts as a historical training culture, not a shopping list;
several are marvels of dedication, a few are marvels of exaggeration, and
almost none belong in year one.

## 1. The Body and the Ground (no equipment — the first "tools")

Stance holds (站桩), the presses and kicks of Part I, and labor itself:
carrying water, sweeping the halls, working the fields (出坡 chūpō). The
first Shaolin training implement is a bucket. This is not romanticism — it
is load carried honestly through a full day, and it is why Part III below
takes household improvisation seriously: the tradition itself is improvised
from farm work.

## 2. Stone Locks — 石锁 shísuǒ

- **What:** carved stone blocks shaped like old Chinese padlocks with a
  handle — the ancestor of the kettlebell, used in Chinese martial arts and
  military examinations for centuries.
- **Trains:** grip, wrist, whole-body power. Classical practice includes
  swings, cleans to the forearm, presses, and — at skill — flips and
  catches (掷石锁 throwing the lock).
- **When:** after the stance foundation holds; in this program, Quarter 2
  onward for swings and carries; flips are a later-years skill.
- **Modern equivalent:** the kettlebell, directly. **DIY:** see Part III.

## 3. The Stone Barbell — 石担 shídàn

Stone wheels mounted on a wooden or bamboo pole — the yard's barbell.
Squats, presses, and shouldered carries. Modern equivalent: any barbell or
a sandbag over the shoulders. Enters when strength work formalizes
(Quarter 2–3 here).

## 4. Sandbags — 沙袋 shādài (two different tools, one name)

**The carrying sandbag (抱沙袋):** a heavy floor bag lifted, shouldered,
carried, and thrown. Trains whole-body strength the way labor does —
awkward, honest load. Enters early (it is just carrying).

**The hanging striking bag (吊沙袋):** the ancestor of the heavy bag —
canvas, traditionally filled with sand *layered with sawdust and cloth* so
it does not set like concrete. Trains striking power, structure on impact,
and footwork around a moving target. **When:** traditionally only after
basics and forms give the strike its shape — in this program, Quarter 3.
Hitting a bag before the stance and the fist are formed just rehearses
errors against resistance.

## 5. The Wall Bag — 打墙包 / and Iron Palm — 铁砂掌 tiěshāzhǎng

- **What:** a flat canvas bag (often three-section) mounted on a wall,
  filled first with **mung beans (绿豆)** — traditionally chosen because
  they are forgiving and, in TCM framing, "cooling"; later gravel or steel
  shot for advanced practitioners. Iron palm training proper uses a flat
  bag on a bench, struck with palm, back-hand, knife-edge, and fingertips
  in slow, relaxed cycles.
- **Trains:** progressive conditioning of the striking surfaces and, more
  importantly, relaxed structure at impact.
- **When:** small doses from Quarter 2–3, *after* pushups and wrist
  strength exist. **The gradualism warning is absolute:** hand conditioning
  is measured in years, done at low intensity with full recovery, and
  stopped at the first sign of joint pain, numbness, or broken skin.
  Deformed knuckles are not achievement; they are error made permanent.
  The traditional liniment culture (跌打酒 diē dǎ jiǔ, "dit da jow") is a
  living tradition worthy of respect — but it is a tradition, not a license
  to overtrain, and no liniment repairs a fractured hand.

## 6. The Striking Post — 木桩 mùzhuāng, and the Arm-Conditioning Tradition

A wooden post, wrapped in rope or padding, kicked and struck with forearms
(the root of 铁臂功 "iron arm"). The famous articulated **wooden dummy
(木人桩 mùrénzhuāng)** belongs primarily to southern systems like Wing
Chun — honor it as its own culture; it is not a Shaolin-yard staple and is
not needed here. A wrapped post or a firm heavy bag covers the same ground
for a first-year practitioner. **When:** Quarter 3+, light contact only.

## 7. Plum-Blossom Poles — 梅花桩 méihuāzhuāng

- **What:** a field of wooden posts set upright in the ground, tops at
  ankle to head height, arranged like plum blossoms. Stances, steps, and
  eventually forms are performed on the pole tops.
- **Trains:** balance, precision footwork, fear management, and total
  commitment of each step — you cannot half-step on a pole.
- **When:** traditionally well after stance work is solid on flat ground.
- **The honest home version is at ground level** (Part III). Height adds
  consequence, not skill; the skill is the precision.

## 8. Gripping Jars — 抓坛子 zhuā tánzi (Eagle Claw — 鹰爪功 yīngzhuǎgōng)

- **What:** wide-mouth clay jars gripped by the fingertips on the rim,
  lifted, carried in horse stance, swung gently. Water, then sand, then
  stones are added over months as the claw strengthens.
- **Trains:** finger, tendon, and claw strength — the gripping arts.
- **Related:** thrusting the fingers into buckets of rice, then beans, then
  sand (插沙 chāshā) — the same progression principle, striking inward.
- **When:** grip work can start gently in Quarter 2; fingertip-intensive
  versions much later. Tendons adapt slower than muscles — years, not weeks.

## 9. The Shoulder Pole — 扁担 biǎndan

Two buckets of water on a springy pole across one shoulder, carried up the
mountain steps without spilling. Trains loaded carrying, trunk stiffness
under rhythm, and patience. This is the most literal "the chore is the
training" tool — and the easiest to reproduce at home exactly.

## 10. Body-Tempering Implements — 排打功 páidǎgōng

Bundled bamboo slats or chopsticks, bean-filled bags, and (much later)
wooden mallets, tapped rhythmically over muscled surfaces of the body to
accustom tissue to impact — the approachable edge of the iron-shirt
(铁布衫 tiěbùshān) tradition. **When:** light self-tapping with a palm or a
bean bag from Quarter 3; sticks and mallets belong to supervised, mature
practice. Never over the spine, throat, kidneys, or joints. The legendary
extremes of iron-shirt demonstrations are performance culture — respect the
training that underlies them, do not reenact the show.

## 11. The Staff and the Weapons Rack — 兵器 bīngqì

The staff (棍 gùn) is *the* Shaolin weapon — the monks' historical fame is
staff fame (少林棍法), and the yard's proverb calls the staff the root of
the eighteen weapons. Traditionally the first weapon taught, and only after
empty-hand basics are sound (a common schoolhouse rule of thumb: basics
first, staff after — often years).

- **The real thing:** a **waxwood staff (白蜡杆 báilàgǎn)** — white wax
  wood is springy, shock-absorbing, and traditional. Sized from eyebrow
  height (齐眉棍 "eyebrow staff") up to head-height-plus-a-fist.
- **First practices:** grip changes, basic strikes (劈 pī chop, 扫 sǎo
  sweep, 戳 chuō thrust), figure-eight flowers (舞花 wǔhuā), and stance
  integration — staff in Bow Stance, staff in Horse Stance.
- **When here:** end of Quarter 2 at the earliest, as a reward for stance
  standards; it doubles as the shoulder-opening bar (压肩) meanwhile.
- Broadsword (刀 dāo), straight sword (剑 jiàn), and spear (枪 qiāng)
  belong to later years. Collecting them early is costume, not practice.

## 12. Iron Rings — 铁环 tiěhuán

Forearm rings worn during form practice for progressive load — strongly
associated with southern systems (Hung Gar's ring-hand sets) though
present across Chinese martial culture. Noted here for completeness and
honesty about origin; wrist weights on slow basics reproduce the idea.
Not a year-one tool: added mass on fast limb movements is a joint tax.

## 13. Clothing and Feet

Loose trousers that survive a full Pu Bu, and flat thin-soled shoes. The
canvas **Feiyue (飞跃)** shoe is the honest, cheap, widely available
standard worn in modern Shaolin training culture. No costume robes; the
manual's "no costume mind" rule stands.

# Part III — The Improvised Training Hall

The tradition itself grew out of farm labor; improvising with household
load is not a compromise of the culture, it is a continuation of it. Every
substitute below preserves the *method*, which is where the art lives.

## Load and Grip

| Traditional | Improvise with | Method preserved |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 石锁 stone lock | a kettlebell; or a sturdy jug/canister filled with wet sand; or a concrete-filled paint bucket with a rebar/pipe handle set in | two-hand swings, one-hand carries, cleans to forearm — same arcs, lighter |
| 石担 stone barbell | duffel bag packed with rice/sand bags across the shoulders | shouldered squats, good-morning hinges, carries |
| 抱沙袋 carrying sandbag | army duffel or sturdy backpack + double-bagged rice or play sand (start 10–15 kg) | bear-hug lifts, shoulder carries, floor-to-shoulder |
| 扁担 shoulder pole | two buckets of water carried by hand (farmer's carry), or a broomstick across the shoulders with light bags tied on | walk the hallway or stairs without spilling — the water is the honest judge |
| 抓坛子 gripping jar | a wide-mouth glass jar or paint can gripped by the rim with fingertips; add water weekly | fingertip lifts and horse-stance carries |
| 插沙 rice-hand bucket | a deep bucket of dry rice; thrust open fingers in, grab, twist, release | grip endurance; move to beans only when rice is easy for months |
| pull/row (Phase 2) | backpack rows, towel rows on a solid door, or a doorway pull-up bar | pulling strength the pushup can't give |

## Striking and Tempering

| Traditional | Improvise with | Method preserved |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 打墙包 wall bag | a canvas or denim bag (an old jeans leg sewn flat works) filled with **mung beans**, hung or wall-mounted at shoulder height | palm and fist placement work, LOW intensity, counted, both hands |
| 铁砂掌 iron palm bench bag | the same bean bag laid flat on a stool | the four-strike cycle: palm, back-hand, knife-edge, fingertip touch — relaxed, dropped, never muscled |
| 吊沙袋 hanging bag | until purchase: a duffel stuffed with rags/old clothes hung from a beam or heavy branch | structure on contact, footwork circles — rag fill is joint-friendly |
| 木桩 striking post | a 4×4 post or tree wrapped thickly in rope or old carpet | light forearm rolls and kicks, gradual |
| 排打 tempering tools | your own palm, then a small bean bag, tapped in lines over thigh, belly, forearm | rhythm and breath with the tap ("exhale on contact"), never bone, never spine |

## Balance and Precision

| Traditional | Improvise with | Method preserved |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 梅花桩 plum-blossom poles | flat bricks, pavers, or chalk/tape circles laid in the plum pattern on the ground | stance transitions landing only on the marks; when a step misses, it "fell" |
| pole endurance | a curb, a 4×4 laid flat, a balance beam line on the floor | one-leg stands (独立步) and slow walking with eyes ahead |

## Flexibility and Structure

| Traditional | Improvise with | Method preserved |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 压腿 press rail | chair back, stair rail, kitchen counter, low bookshelf | all four presses of Part I at whatever height the leg honestly reaches |
| 压肩 shoulder rail | the same chair back or counter | chest pressed through straight arms, spine long |
| 齐眉棍 staff | a hardwood closet dowel or broomstick (until the waxwood staff is bought) | grips, flowers slowed down, shoulder opener, stance-with-staff |
| meditation cushion | firm pillow folded, or rolled blanket | hips above knees in the sit |
| training timer/bell | phone across the room in do-not-disturb, or a kitchen timer | the bell is obeyed, not negotiated with |

**The buckets-of-water rule for all improvised gear:** water and rice are
self-honest loads — they spill, shift, and punish sloppiness the way a
stone lock does and a rubber dumbbell does not. When in doubt, train with
water.

# Part IV — The Purchase List

Bought in the order the training earns them. Nothing on this list is needed
in Quarter 1 except the floor items. Vendors: dedicated Chinese
martial-arts suppliers (Tiger Claw, AWMA, KungFuDirect, Wing Lam
Enterprises) carry the traditional items; general fitness retailers
(Rogue, Title, Ringside, Everlast, or Amazon) cover the modern equivalents.
Check current stock and reviews before ordering; specific models rotate.

## Quarter 1 — the floor (≈ $50–90)

| Item | What kind | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Mat / flooring | puzzle mats or a firm exercise mat, enough for one body length plus a stance width | the Dojo floor |
| Meditation cushion | zafu-style, or keep the folded blanket | comfort is posture |
| Feiyue shoes (飞跃) | canvas, flat sole | the honest training shoe, inexpensive |
| Kitchen timer or small bell | any | the bell of the Temple Day |

## Quarter 2 — grip, load, and the first traditional tools (≈ $100–180)

| Item | What kind | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Kettlebell | cast iron, flat base; a deconditioned adult starts 8–12 kg, adds 16 kg later | the modern 石锁; swings, carries, cleans |
| Wall bag | 3-section canvas wall bag; fill with mung beans from any grocery | mounts to a stud or a board; the striking-surface school |
| Waxwood staff (白蜡杆) | eyebrow-height or taller, from a martial-arts supplier — waxwood specifically, not hardware-store hardwood | doubles as the 压肩 shoulder bar |
| Doorway pull-up bar | any well-reviewed frame-mounted model | rows and hangs; the pulling gap |
| Jump rope | plain speed rope | conditioning between stance rounds |

## Quarter 3 — impact (≈ $120–250)

| Item | What kind | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Heavy bag | hanging, roughly half your body weight (70–100 lb for most adults); canvas or synthetic shell. Traditional canvas 沙袋 from a kung-fu supplier if you want the yard's own tool — fill layered: rags/sawdust core, sand in small proportion only | needs a beam, stand, or wall mount rated for it |
| — or freestanding | Century Wavemaster XXL–class base-filled bag if mounting is impossible | more footprint, less swing |
| Bag gloves / wraps | light bag gloves and cotton wraps for the first months | bare-knuckle canvas work comes gradually, wall-bag first |
| Loose training trousers | any that survive a full Pu Bu | function and humility |

## Quarter 4 and beyond — earned refinements

Second kettlebell (heavier), a true carrying sandbag with handles
(50–80 lb, fill-your-own), paver stones for a proper plum-blossom ground
array, and — only if the staff practice has become real — a second waxwood
staff for partner drills. Dit da jow if you adopt the iron-palm tradition
knowingly, from a martial-arts supplier, labeled for what it is: tradition.

**What not to buy in year one:** a wooden dummy (different system, and
premature), iron rings (joint tax), sword or spear (costume until the
staff is honest), weighted vests for kicks (never), and any "iron shirt /
iron palm secret course." The 72 Arts were never for sale; the counterfeit
versions still aren't.

# The Closing Rule

Every tool above answers to the same audit the manual applies to everything
else: *did it make the daily practice more honest, or did it become
decoration?* A stone lock gathering dust is a rock. A bucket of water
carried up the stairs every morning is 功夫. Buy late, improvise early,
and let the Mirror and the Record — not the receipts — say what you are
becoming.
