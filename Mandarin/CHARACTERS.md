# Characters
## How Hanzi Actually Work, and How to Learn Them by Hand

Characters are not ten thousand random pictures. They are a system built from
a small number of parts, the way forms are built from stances. Learn the parts
and the logic, and every new character becomes a combination you can read,
not a drawing you must memorize.

```text
strokes  →  components/radicals  →  characters  →  words
(~8 basic)   (~200, ~40 matter)      (learn ~600/yr)  (mostly 2-character)
```

Coverage is brutally front-loaded: the most common ~1,000 characters cover
roughly 90 percent of everyday running text. You do not need ten thousand.
You need the right few hundred, learned deeply, by hand.

**Policy for this lane:** simplified characters first (this is what the
mainland, most courses, and most tools teach). Traditional forms are noted
only when a source you use teaches them.

## The Basic Strokes

Every character is a fixed sequence of strokes. There are only a handful:

| Stroke | Name | Motion |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 一 | héng | horizontal, left to right |
| 丨 | shù | vertical, top to bottom |
| 丿 | piě | falling left, curve down-left |
| ㇏ | nà | falling right, press down-right |
| 丶 | diǎn | dot, small press |
| ㇀ | tí | rising flick, lower-left to upper-right |
| 亅 etc. | gōu | hook — attached to the end of another stroke |
| 乛 乚 etc. | zhé | turn — one stroke that bends |

## Stroke Order Rules

Stroke order is not pedantry. It builds the muscle memory that makes writing
fast, makes characters legible, and makes you able to *count strokes* to look
things up. The rules, in priority order:

1. **Top before bottom:** 三 (three horizontal lines, top down).
2. **Left before right:** 川, and all left-radical characters: 你, 好.
3. **Horizontal before vertical** when they cross: 十 (一 then 丨).
4. **Outside before inside:** 月, 同 — frame first, contents second.
5. **Inside before closing the box:** 国, 回 — fill it, then shut the door.
6. **Center before wings** in symmetric characters: 小, 水.
7. **Minor strokes last:** the dot in 玉 comes at the end.

Verify any new character once with [Hanzi Writer](https://hanziwriter.org/)
(see [RESOURCES.md](RESOURCES.md)) before you write it nine times. Nine
repetitions of a wrong order is training a wrong form.

## The Four Kinds of Characters

Knowing *which kind* a character is tells you how to memorize it.

1. **Pictographs** — stylized drawings. 人 person, 山 mountain, 日 sun,
   月 moon, 木 tree, 水 water, 口 mouth, 马 horse. Few, but they become the
   components of everything else.
2. **Simple ideographs** — abstract ideas drawn directly. 一 二 三, 上 up,
   下 down, 中 middle.
3. **Compound ideographs** — meanings stacked. 明 bright = sun 日 + moon 月.
   休 rest = person 亻 leaning on tree 木. 好 good = woman 女 + child 子.
4. **Phono-semantic compounds** — *over 80 percent of all characters.* One
   part hints the meaning (the radical), the other hints the sound.
   妈 mā (mother) = 女 woman (meaning) + 马 mǎ (sound). 吗 ma (question
   particle) = 口 mouth (meaning) + 马 mǎ (sound). Once you know 马, you can
   *guess the pronunciation* of 妈吗骂码蚂. This is the cheat code of the
   entire writing system: learn components, and characters start reading
   themselves.

## The Radicals That Matter

Radicals are the meaning-hinting components and the index keys of every
dictionary. About 40 do most of the work. Learn these as vocabulary in their
own right during months 1-3 — every one of them multiplies future characters.

## The Learning Method (Per Character)

This is the standard for the nightly Evening Hand block and any new character:

```text
1. LOOK IT UP.  CC-CEDICT for pinyin + meaning. Never guess a tone.
2. WATCH IT.    Hanzi Writer stroke-order animation, once.
3. BREAK IT.    Name its components aloud: "妈 = woman + horse."
                If it is phono-semantic, note which part carries the sound.
4. SAY IT.      Aloud, correct tone, 3 times, hand tracing the tone contour.
5. WRITE IT.    9 times, correct stroke order, decreasing attention on the
                model: 3 copying, 3 glancing, 3 from memory.
6. USE IT.      One word or tiny phrase containing it, spoken aloud.
7. CARD IT.     Two Anki cards:
                - Recognition:  character front → pinyin + meaning back
                - Production:   meaning + pinyin front → write the character
                                on paper, then flip to check
```

Component stories are legal and powerful ("a woman 女 beside a horse 马 —
it's *ma*, mother"). Invent them freely to remember; just never confuse a
memory-story with real etymology when you talk about the language.

## Review Protocol

- Anki daily, 10 cards minimum, per the deck rule in
  [CURRICULUM.md](CURRICULUM.md): if the deck swells into a swamp, freeze new
  cards and review only.
- **Handwriting decays fastest.** Once per week (Saturday long session), write
  the current month's characters from memory, cold, on paper. Anything you
  cannot produce goes back into rotation.
- Mistakes are data: a character missed twice gets its components re-broken
  and a new story.

## Milestones for the Year

| Point | Characters (cumulative) | What that buys you |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Month 1 | ~25-30 | anchor characters, numbers, radicals begin |
| Month 3 | ~100 | radical set + training vocabulary readable |
| Month 6 | ~250-300 | simple sentences readable with pinyin support |
| Month 9 | ~450 | graded-reader entry level |
| Month 12 | ~600 | the practice glossary, journal fully anchored, HSK2-3 range |

These are honest numbers for 15-20 minutes daily plus the Saturday session —
not the inflated counts of app marketing. Depth over count: a character you
can write, say, and use beats three you can only vaguely recognize.
